02   Problem Discovery

How I found the problem

It started as a personal observation. The question was: is this one person's experience — or a documented behavioural pattern?

The trigger

A daily YouTube user watched on her husband's account and accidentally discovered Turkish village cooking content. She loved every video. Back on her own account — nothing. She tried to search for it. Didn't know what to search for. That moment of lost discovery is the problem.

"I start searching hopelessly, mostly not finding anything new. Show me stuff I have not seen before — and based on my activity, show it to me on a frequency that suits me."

Secondary research — what the evidence says

Mozilla Research, 2022
YouTube's "Not Interested" signal only prevents 11% of bad recommendations. Even "Don't Recommend Channel" — the most powerful tool — only cuts 43%.
Interpretation: YouTube's feedback tools barely work. The system is designed to ignore what users say they don't want.
User forums (ResetEra)
"When I research a product, I get a non-stop deluge for the next four months — with no way to tell YouTube I've already bought it."
Interpretation: The algorithm doesn't know when an interest is satisfied. It reinforces forever.
MDPI Systematic Review, 2025 (30 studies)
Algorithmic systems structurally amplify ideological homogeneity, reinforcing selective exposure and limiting viewpoint diversity.
Interpretation: This is structural, not accidental. The algorithm is doing exactly what it's designed to do.
Platform data
YouTube's recommendation algorithm drives 70% of all watch time. The homepage is where the majority of viewing decisions happen.
Interpretation: The algorithm's narrowing effect isn't a side effect — it's the primary experience for most users, most of the time.
The validated pattern

Users who watch YouTube heavily develop narrow recommendation profiles — not by choice, but by algorithm design. There is no native mechanism to signal openness or curiosity. Users who want to escape the loop resort to workarounds: incognito mode, using a partner's account, or switching to TikTok. That last one is the retention risk.

04   Competitive Landscape

What everyone else is doing

Every major competitor has either solved for passive novelty or built user-prompted discovery. YouTube — with the world's largest content library — has neither.

Platform Discovery mechanism Proactive discovery? User agency?
TikTok For You Page — interest-agnostic, surfaces novel content by default regardless of history ✓ Solved ✗ None
Spotify Discover Weekly + AI Prompted Playlists — user types a mood or theme, AI builds around it ✓ Solved ✓ Strong
Netflix "Play Something" — tried passive random shuffle in 2021, removed 2023 due to low usage ~ Tried & failed ✗ Random ≠ agency
Instagram Reels "Your Algorithm" — users edit which topics influence their feed. Reactive, not proactive. ✗ Reactive only ~ Partial
YouTube "Not Interested" + "Don't Recommend Channel" — feedback signals that barely work (11–43% effectiveness) ✗ None ✗ None
The gap no one has filled:

Netflix's "Play Something" failure is the signal. Users don't want random — they want intelligently surprising. A mechanism that combines AI inference (detecting disengagement passively) with micro-category specificity (not broad genres — Turkish Village Cooking, not "Food"). That's exactly what Uncharted is.

05   Problem Prioritisation

Why this problem, why now

PIF Framework — Population × Intensity × Frequency. Discovery scores maximum across all three dimensions.

5
Population

Affects every regular user. Not a segment — this is the core homepage experience.

5
Intensity

Drives silent platform abandonment. Users don't complain — they open TikTok instead.

5
Frequency

Every session. Every homepage load. The narrowing compounds over time.

15/15
Maximum score — P0
Regular YouTube users who have outgrown their algorithmic profile have no way to signal curiosity, mood, or openness to the unknown — and no way to escape their own watch history. They lack a mechanism to discover content that could change the way they think, unless they get lucky.
06   The Solution

Uncharted — design decisions

Every design decision has a reason. None of these were defaults.

Decision
Micro-category capsules, not individual videos
Why
Individual videos carry creator/production risk. A capsule lets the user assess the entire topic before committing. Lower barrier to curiosity.
Decision
Feed update only after 2+ videos watched in a category
Why
Earned preference, not assumed. One video could be accidental. Two is signal. Prevents the algorithm re-narrowing based on a single click.
Decision
"+ More" shuffles 3 capsules, not all 7
Why
Partial shuffle preserves anchoring — known capsules stay visible while allowing exploration. Reduces the cost of trying again.
Decision
Named "Uncharted" not "What's New" or "Beyond"
Why
"What's New" implies recency, not novelty. "Beyond" is vague. "Uncharted" captures the feeling of going somewhere unmapped — sets the right expectation.
Decision
Non-intrusive banner entry point — dismissable, no penalty
Why
Opt-in discovery. Doesn't disrupt users who are satisfied with their current feed. Respects that disengagement may be temporary.

AI signals — what triggers Uncharted

✓ Scroll without play (3+ videos skipped) ~ Background watching (20+ min, no interaction) ✓ Return after 72h+ inactivity ✓ Category saturation (80%+ same 2 topics, 30 days)

Requires 2+ signals to fire. Hard frequency cap: max once per 48 hours per user.

Guardrails — these must not decline

Core feed engagement rate and overall session length must not decline in the A/B cohort. If either moves negatively, the feature is paused before broader rollout.

09   Pre-Mortem

What could go wrong

Assume this ships. Six months later it's pulled. What happened?

🐯 Tiger — real problem
Taxonomy too broad

Capsules feel like "cooking" not "Turkish village cooking." Users don't feel the discovery.

Mitigation: Minimum specificity criteria. Audit 50 capsules with real users in beta before launch.
🐯 Tiger — real problem
Signal fires too aggressively

Users who are just multitasking get interrupted constantly.

Mitigation: Require 2+ concurrent signals. Hard frequency cap: max 1x per 48h per user.
🐘 Elephant — unspoken risk
Discovery without adoption

Users enjoy Uncharted sessions but never add categories to feed. It becomes a novelty, not a behaviour change.

Detection: Feed confirmation rate <20% is the early warning. Reconsider the adoption UX before scaling.
🐯 Tiger — but manageable
Cannibalism of core feed

Users spend time in Uncharted instead of — not in addition to — their existing session.

Mitigation: Guardrail metric explicitly covers this. A/B test with 5% cohort first. Monitor session length delta.
11   What I'd Validate Next

The questions shipping would answer

Taxonomy quality audit

Show 20 users 50 micro-category capsules. Ask: does this feel specific enough to be interesting? The answer determines whether the taxonomy framework is right before engineering builds it.

Trigger calibration diary study

Run a 2-week diary study with 10 users. Ask them to flag moments of boredom or stuckness on YouTube. Map to session event data. Validate whether our 4 signals actually correlate with self-reported disengagement.

Adoption threshold A/B test

Test the 2-video trigger vs. 1-video vs. explicit user-initiated "Add to feed." Is 2 the right threshold — or does explicit intent produce higher quality adoption?

Creator ecosystem impact

Does Uncharted divert watch time to smaller, niche creators — or just resurface YouTube's existing long-tail in a new UI? This determines whether YouTube can pitch Uncharted as a creator growth tool too.

Global content quality check

Run the taxonomy against non-English content at scale. The discovery gap may be most acute in markets where the algorithm has the least data — India, Southeast Asia, Latin America. If Uncharted works better there, that's the launch market.

YouTube shouldn't wait for users to get lucky.

Uncharted makes discovery a design decision, not a happy accident.
